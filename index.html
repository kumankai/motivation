Intrinsic Motivation

How to Find and Nurture it

Intrinsic motivation holds a force that propels a person to partake in activities out of their comfort zone, amusement, or personal fulfillment. Unlike extrinsic motivators that rely on external rewards, intrinsic motivation comes from within, connected to a whole sense of purpose, enthusiasm or curiosity.

There are many levels to this internal drive that impacts the way people behave and achieve things. The very center of intrinsic motivation starts with being fulfilled from the activities they are engaging in, which also involves connecting with their personal beliefs, values and goals. This sense allows a person to pursue goals just to experience the activity itself. Whether it's finding a new hobby, perfecting a skill, or continuing a project they are passionate about, intrinsic motivation pushes people forward with a sense of purpose and excitement.

Nurturing this intrinsic motivation is important since it allows a person to gain a stronger connection to their creativity and passions, rather than temporary incentives.

Ways to find and nurture Intrinsic Motivation:

    • Reflect on Interests: Recognize activities that make you enthusiastic and keep you interested.
    • Have Meaningful Goals: Define clear goals aligned with values and aspirations.
    • Look for freedom: Take control in the decisions.
    • Welcoming Challenges: Go for opportunities that allow growth and learn from the mistakes along the way.
    • Connect to Purpose: Find a bigger meaning and impact for the activities you participate in.
    • Curiosity drives sparks: Look at activities with a spirit of wonder and adventure.
    • Celebrate accomplishments: Recognize and celebrate your achievements and the journey to them.
    • Create a Supportive Environment: Keep people that encourage you to excel in your passions.
    • Practice Self-Care: Make your well-being a priority in order to sustain motivation.
    • Reflect and modify: Constantly review and change the ways you maintain motivation.

Extrinsic Motivation

How is extrinsic Motivation used in the Workforce?

1.Targeting the Right Rewards:

Why it matters: People are motivated by different things. What excites one employee might leave another completely uninspired. A generic approach to rewards won't maximize their potential. Some employees might crave financial stability and security, making salary increases or performance-based bonuses highly effective. Others might thrive on recognition and social validation - for them, public praise, awards, or even a prestigious job title could be far more motivating. Some employees might value flexibility and work-life balance, so perks like extra time off or work-from-home options could be the key to their motivation.

How to implement: Understanding these individual motivations requires open communication with your employees. Informal conversations, structured surveys, and dedicated one-on-one meetings can help you uncover what drives each person on your team. Tailor your extrinsic rewards accordingly. For example, if an employee expresses a desire for professional development, create opportunities for them to attend conferences or training programs. If another employee shares the stress of work-life balance, consider offering flexible work hours or remote working options. This personalized approach will make your extrinsic motivators more powerful and prevent them from feeling like empty gestures.

2.Balancing Extrinsic with Intrinsic Motivation:

Why it matters in-depth: Extrinsic motivation, while useful for short-term goals or sparking initial engagement, can only take you so far. If employees feel that their work is solely a means to an end (a paycheck, a promotion, etc.), they're more likely to become disengaged and lose sight of the value of their work. Intrinsic motivation - the desire to do something because of its inherent satisfaction and meaning - is a far more sustainable drive for long-term success.

How to do it in-depth: Cultivating intrinsic motivation alongside extrinsic rewards is key. Ensure that employees understand how their work contributes to the company's mission and overall goals. This helps them find meaning in their tasks. Provide opportunities for growth and professional development, allowing employees to build knowledge and feel a sense of progress. Grant employees as much autonomy as possible, allowing them to control aspects of their work and take ownership of projects. This sense of responsibility and control can be intrinsically motivating. When combined with a sense of purpose, skill development, and autonomy, extrinsic rewards become powerful additions. They reinforce achievement and progress, but they aren't the sole reason for an employee's dedication.

Examples of Extrinsic Motivation

  • Financial rewards: Financial rewards such as salaries, bonuses, and commissions, offer external incentives for performance improvement. For example, salespeople may strive to close more deals to earn higher commissions.
  • Promotions: Promotions serve as recognition for hard work, offering increased responsibilities, prestige, and often, higher salaries. Employees are motivated to prove themselves worthy of advancement within the company.
  • Praise and recognition: Praise and recognition fulfill the basic human need for appreciation and validation. Whether through a simple "good job" from a manager or public acknowledgment of achievements, this extrinsic motivator can drive employees to excel.
  • Company perks: Company perks, like free lunches or gym memberships, enhance job attractiveness and encourage employee retention. While improving well-being and satisfaction, these benefits remain external to the core work.
  • punishment: Avoiding punishment, such as reprimands or job loss, acts as a strong extrinsic motivator. Employees may strive to meet deadlines or avoid mistakes to maintain their manager's approval and job security.

Managerial Practices

Utilize managerial role and practices.

Effective management within organizations relies on several critical practices that leaders must implement. Clear communication of goals and strategies is paramount to aligning employee efforts with overarching objectives. Alongside this, implementing quality control mechanisms ensures consistent standards across products and processes, fostering efficiency and excellence.

The landscape of knowledge work has undergone significant evolution, with a notable shift towards remote settings, as highlighted by Emergent Research's findings that two-thirds of such work now occurs beyond traditional offices. Despite efforts to design collaborative office environments, the ascendancy of digital work and decentralized collaboration poses a distinct challenge. While digital tools facilitate interactions and idea exchange, studies emphasize the enduring importance of face-to-face engagement. Evidence suggests that remote teams often lag behind their physically co-located counterparts, as evidenced by sociometric badge studies.

In response, workers are reshaping their work settings, leading to the rise of coworking spaces. By 2013, data from Emergent Research indicated that over 160,000 individuals were utilizing thousands of coworking spaces in the United States and Europe. Additionally, surveys conducted in 2014 revealed that 72% of respondents anticipated an income increase. Research indicates that coworking spaces contribute to heightened productivity, expanded professional networks, decreased isolation, and increased trust among members. The growing embrace of coworking signifies a broader trend towards adaptable work environments tailored to individual preferences and work methodologies, reflecting a shift in how and where work is conducted.

How to utilize managerial practices to foster creativity and productivity:

    • Engaging and empowering employees fosters innovation and continuous improvement
    • Frontlining insights are crucial for identifying areas of enhancement
    • Regular check-ins with employees encourage open communication and feedback
    • Leveraging technology optimizes operations for focusing on self initiatives
